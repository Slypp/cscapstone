    Case Study


Root Cause #1

There is a lack of data that prevents researchers from understanding the quantity and diversity of undocumented students in college

 Root Cause #2

There is a lack of information and encouragement to undocumented students once they realize they are not eligible for FSA.
Evidence #1: “In addition to data limitations, existing research primarily focuses on understanding the experiences of Latino youth, and as a result, we know relatively little about the experiences of non-Latino undocumented youth. Much of what we know about undocumented youth comes from qualitative studies that focus on very specific subpopulations” Evidence #1:“Many do not know that they can pursue higher education: whether that is from lack of resources to be ready for the application process, disempowerment and discrimination in their schooling experience, or simply because they do not see themselves excelling in that area and decide to take front-line jobs to sustain themselves and/or their families.”
Explanation 1: There is a lack of data of other immigrant populations because most of the information that is available now is mainly focused on hispanic students. This lack of data prevents a performance comparison between undocumented students. Explanation 1: Undocumented students face more issues than students with legal status because they believe college is expensive and they are putting themselves at risk by simply attending a college. Because of this and many other personal things, they decide to join the workforce because they believe that is the only way to survive.
Evidence #2:“Our understanding of how legal status affects the educational experiences of undocumented students in college has been limited by data constraints primarily because large-scale representative surveys in the United States do not collect data on legal status (e.g., American Community Survey, National Longitudinal Survey of Youth, Educational Longitudinal Survey, etc.).” /td> Evidence #2:“This is the year when most students find out that they don't qualify for FAFSA and the majority of scholarships. First because they don't have a social security number and second because they are not "legal" permanent residents or citizens of the United States. Many students by this point are discouraged and don't believe that going to college is a possibility.”
Explanation #2: There is not a specific number of undocumented students attending college because there is no Survey that asks for their legal status, but because of this, it makes it harder for researchers to compare the numbers of undocumented students and students with legal status. This lack of information also makes it hard to analyze the experiences of undocumented students in college. Explanation #2: High Schools fail to provide information regarding student aid, especially for undocumented students, which consequently leads to students being discouraged and not even applying to colleges.
IMPLICATION FOR BOTH ROOT CAUSES:
There is a lack of information over all, students do not receive the help they need, there is no information on other undocumented students besides Latinos, and students are not encouraged to apply to college. Also, it is hard to know how to help students because you there is no way to know who is undocumented and who is not, which consequently makes it difficult to find a solution to the problem.
